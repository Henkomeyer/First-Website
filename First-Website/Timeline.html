The Historical Timeline of the Internet

1969

Arpanet is run for the first time
Computer clients from Stanford University and the University of California connect to a network via packet switching technology.
Unix is born
An operating system which would later serve as the predecessor to Linux and FreeBSD.

1970

Arpanet Network established
Harvard University, the Massachusetts Institution of Technology and Raytheon BBN (Bolt Beranek and Newman) Technologies connect to each other via an interface developed by the latter, the interface message processor.

1971

Email Technology developed
Ray Tomlinson develops email technology and decides the ‘@’ symbol will separate the user’s name from their computer’s name (later to be replaced by the domain’s name).

Project Gutenberg and Development of eBooks starts
Project Gutenberg is launched in an effort to make books and reading material available to the world in a digital format. This was, for all intents and purposes, the dawn of eBook technology.

1972

CYCLADES launches
A French interpretation of Arpanet ushers in the idea that data transmission should be monitored and administrated by the host computer, instead of the network.

1973

Email Popularity rises
In this year, emails represented for three quarters of all Arpanet activity.
First Trans-Atlantice Connection
An Arpanet connection is established with the University College of London.

1974

The Beginning of TCP/IP
Vinton Cerf, Yogen Dalal and Carl Sunshine propose that Arpa-like networks be connected into a, so-called, “inter-network” which would be operated by the Transmission Control Protocol (later becoming TCP/IP).

1975

First Modern Email Client
A Southern Californian programmer, John Vittal, develops the first modern email program known as MSG. It introduced forwarding and replying to emails.

1977

First PC Modem
Dennis Hayes and Dale Heatherington develop the first PC modem for sale to computer enthusiasts.

1978

Bulletin Board System (BBS) introduced
A whiteout in the city of Chicago drives programmers to develop the first Bulletin Board System.

Spam is born
Gary Thuerk sends unsolicited commercial email messages (which would later come to be known as spam messages) to over 600 Chicago Arpanet users.

1979

The Earliest form of Multiplayer games.
MultiUser Dungeon (MUD) games are developed and featured text-based virtual settings, role-playing, interaction, fiction and online communication. MUDs would later go on to inspire games like the Warcraft series of video games.

Usenet introduced
Developed by two college students, Usenet was an internet-based discussion platform which allowed users across the world to communicate about certain topics grouped by newsgroups.

1980

ENQUIRE Software is launched
CERN launches ENQUIRE, which is a software developed by Tim Berners-Lee, that allows users to keep track of employees, resources and projects using hyperlinks.

1982

The First ASCII Emoticon
Scott Falhman proposes typing or posting “:-)" after making a joke, and thusly ushers in the modern emoticon.

1983

Arpanet Computers convert to TCP/IP
The deadline for conversion from Arpanet to Vincent Cerf’s TCP/IP was scheduled for the first of January and affected hundreds of computers.

1984

Domain Name System (DNS) introduced
The Domain Name System and subsequently Domain Name Servers made addresses easier to access and understand by human beings.

1985

The First Virtual Communities
Stewart Brand and Larry Brilliant develop one of the oldest still running virtual communities named “The WELL” (Whole Earth eLectronic Link) for the Whole Earth Review.

1986

Beginning of the Protocol Wars
Supported by European countries, Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) competes with the United States backed Internet/Arpanet Protocol, the latter of which being triumphant.

1987

Userbase Increase
Since the conversion from arpanet to TCP/IP, the number of supportable hosts rose from the original 1,000 to more than 30,000 (the total number of hosts in 1987).

1988

Internet Relay Chat (IRC) introduced
Developed and introduced as a means for users to chat in real-time, becoming the predecessor of most modern instant messaging programs.

First Major Malicious Internet-based Attack
Robert Tappan Morris wrote and unleashed “The Morris Worm” onto the internet, causing many large interruptions throughout the internet.

1989

AOL is deployed
Formerly known as AppleLink but renamed to America Online after Apple’s retraction from the project, popularized the internet with casual internet users.

Tim Berners-Lee proposes the World Wide Web
As mentioned in the previous chapter, Tim Berners-Lee proposed the idea of the web whilst employed at CERN.

1990

First Commercial dial-up ISP begins
“The World” was the first of its kind, and also marked the end of Arpanet.

World Wide Web Protocols are finished
Based upon Tim Berners-Lee's 1989 code, standards were written for HTML, HTTP and URLs.

1991

First Web Page is created
Much like the very first email sent, the very first web page’s sole purpose was to inform readers about itself and the World Wide Web.

First Content-based Search Protocol implemented
Gopher launches to examine file contents instead of exclusively file names.

MP3 becomes Standard
After becoming standardized, the highly compressable file format became widely used to store and transfer music (from individual songs to whole albums) over the internet.

The First Webcam is deployed
Inside a computer lab at Cambridge University the first webcam was deployed with the goal of monitoring a coffee maker so that those inside the lab did not have to travel to an empty coffee pot.

1993

The First widely downloaded Internet Browser is launched
Mosaic, despite not being the very first internet browser, was the first graphical and easily accessible browser for users unfamiliar with technology.

Governments make an online presence
The United States and the United Nations create webpages on the internet, spawning new domain names like “.gov” and “.org”

1994

Internet Browser options increase
Netscape Navigator is launched in direct competition with Mosaic

1995

Commercialization of the Internet
Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) is developed to secure online financial transactions, inspiring many to create digital commerce enterprises. This also marks the year that what would later become eBay and Amazon start up.

Geocities and The Vatican makes an online presence
Geocities (a web hosting service) was launched (and was shut down in late 2009), in addition to the Vatican creating their own website.

JavaScript and ActiveX introduced
LiveScript, developed by Brendan Eich and later renamed JavaScript, was made available. Microsoft would launch ActiveX the following year.

1996

First Web-based Email Service
HoTMaiL (with the name being a nod to Tim Berner-Lee's HTML) is introduced as the first webmail service.

1997

“Weblog” used as a term
Despite not being the first of their kind, these blogs were the first to be called by the term “weblog”.

1998

First use of online media to ‘break’ a story
Nicknamed the “Monicagate” scandal, the first large news article to be published online was the scandal between Bill Clinton and Monica Lewinsky, posted on The Drudge Report after it was taken down by Newsweek.

Google! Launches
The popular internet browser we know today was introduced and refashioned the way information is retrieved online.

Internet-based file-sharing software is developed
Napster, a peer-to-peer file sharing software (more often than not audio files encoded as MP3 files), is launched

1999

SETI@home project begins
Using the computing power of about three million computers (when they are idle) across the globe, the creators seek to analyze radio telescope data for signs of extraterrestrial life.

2000

The Dotcom Collapse occurs
Following a period of massive growth, stocks in technology companies crashed, resulting in many companies being shut down and the NASDAQ losing 10% of its value in a 24-hour time period.

2001

Wikipedia is launched
Wikipedia’s inception created the foundation for collective web resource generation and online social media.

2003

Voice over Internet Protocol (VoIP) becomes popularized
Aiming to provide an accessible interface for VoIP calling other users, Skype is launched.

MySpace becomes the dominant social media network
Although having been surpassed by Facebook in recent times, MySpace became every increasingly popular and it’s userbase increased exponentially.

CAN-SPAM Act is formed
The Controlling the Assault of Non-Solicited Pornography and Marketing Act is signed by the United States Congress.

2004

The Inception of Web 2.0
Conferences are held discussing Rich Internet Applications (RIA) that are highly interactive and driven by the user. At one such conference, it was discussed that software should take advantage of the online connectivity and become independent from user’s computers.

Social Media rises and Digg is launched
“Social Media” as a term was first coined by Chris Sharpley, at the same time that websites and applications surrounding the topic were getting a lot of traction. One of these earlier sites, a precursor to Reddit, Yahoo!, etc. was Digg.

“The” Facebook launches
The social media giant as we know it today launched in early 2004, and was only available to college students before later expanding.

2005

Streaming video is popularized
Free online video streaming is brought to the public through Youtube.

2006

Twitter launches
Twitter (almost spelt twttr in commemoration of Flickr) launches in the middle of 2006 with the first message to the public being “just setting up my twttr”.

2007

TV Shows try to move online
One of the industry’s first attempts at moving television online was with the launch of Hulu by ABC, NBC and Fox.

iPhone and Mobile Web are developed
The first iPhone is released into the world and ushers in an increasing interest into mobile web applications and development.

2008

Internet Elections are held
The first partly online election took place in the United States, and was the year Barack Obama was elected as president of the United States of America. At this time, it was instrumental for a candidate’s campaign to have some sort of social media presence or platform to speak from.

2009

ICANN Policy changes
The United States lessened its tight grip on the policies of ICANN, the organization responsible for registering online domain names.
